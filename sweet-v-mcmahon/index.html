Sweet v. McMahon — The Borrower Defense Class Action

A nationwide class settlement that delivered approximately $6 billion in borrower defense relief to roughly 200,000 federal student-loan borrowers. The settlement, approved by the Northern District of California in November 2022 and upheld by the Ninth Circuit, automatically discharged loans associated with specified schools without requiring individual applications.

Background

Sweet v. Cardona (later Sweet v. McMahon under the renamed Department of Education leadership) was filed in 2019 by borrowers whose Borrower Defense to Repayment applications had stagnated at the Department of Education. The class consisted of all federal-loan borrowers who had filed a Borrower Defense application before June 22, 2022, and whose application had not received a final decision.

The settlement structure

The November 16, 2022 settlement created three buckets:

  • Settlement-class members from listed schools — approximately 200,000 borrowers received automatic relief (full discharge of federal student loans associated with the listed schools, plus refunds of past payments). The list named approximately 150 schools where evidence of misconduct was strong; Corinthian Colleges, Westwood, Brightwood, Education Corporation of America, and Argosy/Art Institutes are on it.
  • Settlement-class members not from listed schools — the Department of Education committed to deciding their Borrower Defense applications within specific timeframes (with rolling deadlines) and refunding amounts paid if granted.
  • Post-class members (filed after June 22, 2022) — not covered by the settlement; Borrower Defense application processed under the standard rules.

What this means for current borrowers

Check whether your school is on the Sweet exhibit list. If yes:

  • Your federal loans (FFEL or Direct, associated with the listed school) are automatically discharged or in process of being discharged.
  • You should see updates in your studentaid.gov account and from your loan servicer.
  • Refunds of past payments on the discharged loans are also part of the settlement.

If you filed Borrower Defense before June 22, 2022 and your school is not on the list, the Department is on a court-supervised schedule to decide your application.

Sweet does not foreclose other discharge paths

If your school is not on the Sweet list and your Borrower Defense was denied, you may still pursue § 523(a)(8) discharge in bankruptcy under the Brunner test or Department of Justice / Education attestation guidance. Sweet eligibility and § 523(a)(8) eligibility are independent.

The Ninth Circuit affirmance

Several intervenor schools appealed the settlement, arguing the Department exceeded its authority. The Ninth Circuit affirmed in 2023, allowing relief to proceed. Discharges under the settlement have been distributed in ongoing batches.

Where to check status

  • studentaid.gov — account status, loan-by-loan
  • Project on Predatory Student Lending — class counsel; settlement updates
  • Your loan servicer's website (Mohela, Aidvantage, Nelnet, etc.)
