Federal Student Loan Servicers in Bankruptcy Litigation

When a borrower files an adversary proceeding under § 523(a)(8), the named defendant is generally the loan holder, not the servicer. But servicer behavior — in litigation, in negotiation, and in pre-AP collection — varies meaningfully. This page covers the major federal loan holders and their AP posture.

Loan holder vs servicer — the distinction

Federal student loans have two roles:

  • Holder: the entity that owns the debt. For most Direct Loans this is the U.S. Department of Education itself. For older FFEL loans it may be a private guaranty agency (e.g., ECMC) that took the loan after default under FFEL guarantee terms.
  • Servicer: the entity that handles billing, customer service, and routine collection on behalf of the holder. Mohela, Aidvantage, Nelnet, EdFinancial, Default Resolution Group are common servicers.

Adversary proceedings name the holder, not the servicer. Service of process goes to the holder's registered agent.

The major holders

U.S. Department of Education (Direct Loans)

Holder of nearly all post-2010 federal loans. Defended through the Department of Justice's Civil Division (Federal Programs Branch) when AP litigation occurs. Since November 17, 2022 has applied attestation guidance favorably in many qualifying cases.

ECMC — Educational Credit Management Corporation

The largest guaranty agency for FFEL defaulted loans. Notoriously aggressive in defending § 523(a)(8) APs — ECMC's litigation posture pre-2022 was a major reason the empirical AP filing rate was so low. ECMC routinely litigates to judgment and appeals adverse rulings.

ECMC's litigation has softened somewhat under the post-2022 guidance climate but remains the most adversarial federal-loan defendant in AP practice.

Other guaranty agencies (FFEL legacy)

USA Funds, American Education Services (AES), Pennsylvania Higher Education Assistance Agency (PHEAA), and others. Posture varies; PHEAA tends to be moderate; some smaller agencies are inactive.

What this means for AP strategy

  • Identify the holder, not the servicer. Pull your studentaid.gov account or NSLDS data to see who actually holds each loan.
  • Anticipate ECMC differently from Department of Education. ECMC will defend; Department of Education may attest if the November 2022 guidance applies.
  • Address all holders separately. If your loans are held by multiple entities (e.g., some Direct Loans + some ECMC FFEL), each is a separate defendant with separate counsel and separate posture.
