Pro Se § 523(a)(8) Filing Guide

Filing a § 523(a)(8) adversary proceeding without a lawyer is procedurally available. This page walks through the procedural framework. It is educational, not legal advice, and does not substitute for consultation with a licensed bankruptcy attorney.

Threshold: should you file pro se?

Most bankruptcy courts allow individuals to represent themselves in their own case (pro se). Corporate or business entities cannot represent themselves — they must have counsel under Rowland v. California Men's Colony, 506 U.S. 194 (1993). For an individual filing a § 523(a)(8) AP on their own behalf, pro se is procedurally available, but the AP is genuinely complex litigation.

Honest factors weighing against pro se filing:

  • Substantive Brunner-test (or totality-of-circumstances) law is jurisdiction-specific and fact-intensive.
  • Procedural errors at AP filing or during discovery can result in dismissal or unfavorable default.
  • The Department of Education and private lenders are represented by counsel familiar with the procedure.
  • Documentary evidence (income records, expense records, repayment history, medical records where applicable) needs to be assembled to a litigation standard.

Honest factors weighing in favor of pro se filing in some cases:

  • The Nov 2022 DOJ guidance has materially reduced the procedural complexity of federal-loan APs where DOJ stipulates to discharge.
  • The cost of representation is real, and § 523(a)(8) AP work is often outside a standard bankruptcy attorney's flat-fee scope.
  • Many bankruptcy courts and law school clinics have pro se assistance programs.

Procedural framework (high level)

  1. You must already have a bankruptcy case open. A § 523(a)(8) AP is filed inside an existing Chapter 7, Chapter 13, or other bankruptcy case. If your bankruptcy case is closed, you generally need to reopen it to file the AP.
  2. Draft the complaint. The complaint identifies the parties (you as plaintiff, the loan holder/servicer as defendant), states the basis (§ 523(a)(8) and the applicable circuit's undue-hardship standard), and pleads facts supporting each element of the test. The complaint must comply with Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7008 (incorporating Rule 8 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure).
  3. File the complaint. Filing fee is typically $350. Some courts allow fee waivers for indigent filers via 28 U.S.C. § 1915.
  4. Serve the defendants. Service requirements differ by defendant. The Department of Education must be served per Rule 7004(b)(4) or (5) (typically certified mail to the U.S. Attorney's Office for the district AND the U.S. Department of Justice in Washington). Private lenders are served under Rule 7004(h) for FDIC-insured institutions or Rule 7004(b) otherwise.
  5. Defendant's answer. Defendants have 30 days from service to answer or move to dismiss. The U.S. has 35 days under Rule 7012(a).
  6. Discovery. Both sides exchange documents, may take depositions, and may serve interrogatories. Discovery is governed by Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure 7026–7037.
  7. Pre-trial proceedings. Most courts require a pre-trial conference, status reports, and proposed scheduling orders.
  8. Trial or stipulation. If DOJ stipulates to discharge under the Nov 2022 guidance for federal loans, the case may end without trial. Otherwise, a trial is held; the court issues findings of fact and conclusions of law and then enters judgment.

Court-specific resources

Every U.S. bankruptcy court publishes pro se filing resources on its website. Common resources:

  • Pro se filer's handbook (often a PDF on the court's website)
  • Sample complaint and answer templates
  • Local rules (LBR) supplementing the federal rules
  • Self-help desk hours where clerk staff can answer procedural (not substantive) questions
  • Many districts maintain a list of legal-aid organizations and law-school clinics that accept pro bono § 523(a)(8) cases

Documentation you'll need

Assemble these before drafting the complaint:

  • Loan records: original promissory notes, current servicer records, full payment history, correspondence with the lender/servicer
  • Income records: tax returns for the past 3–5 years, pay stubs, IDR application history, benefit award letters (Social Security, disability, etc.)
  • Expense records: bank statements, rent/mortgage records, medical bills, any other recurring expenses establishing the minimal-standard-of-living analysis
  • Repayment-effort records: applications for IDR, deferment, forbearance; correspondence documenting hardship; PSLF certifications if applicable
  • Medical/disability records where the persistence prong involves a medical or disability basis
If at any point this seems like a lot: it is. Most successful pro se § 523(a)(8) filers have either a strong document trail to start with or substantial time to assemble one before filing. Most law school bankruptcy clinics will assist pro se filers; many state bar referral services include reduced-fee or sliding-scale bankruptcy counsel; and DOJ's post-2022 guidance has made some federal-loan APs effectively procedural exercises rather than full litigation. Find out what's available before deciding to do this entirely alone.

Common procedural mistakes to avoid

  • Filing the AP in the wrong case (or before opening a bankruptcy case at all)
  • Naming the wrong defendant (e.g., the servicer when the holder is what § 523(a)(8) requires)
  • Improper service on the United States (the Department of Education)
  • Missing the 30/35-day window to respond to a motion or answer
  • Failing to comply with local rules on pre-trial submissions
  • Pleading the Brunner factors as conclusions rather than supported by specific facts
