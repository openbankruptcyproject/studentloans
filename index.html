Student Loan Discharge in Bankruptcy

A plain-language overview of how student loans interact with bankruptcy — what the law says, what changed in November 2022, and what paths to discharge actually exist. This page is educational, not legal advice.

Important: Student loan dischargeability is one of the most fact-specific and case-specific areas of bankruptcy law. Nothing on this page is a recommendation about your situation. If you are considering bankruptcy, talk to a licensed bankruptcy attorney in your jurisdiction.

The basic rule

Federal bankruptcy law — specifically 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8) — makes student loans presumptively non-dischargeable. Filing for bankruptcy does not, on its own, eliminate student loan debt. To discharge a student loan in bankruptcy, the debtor must file a separate proceeding (called an adversary proceeding, or "AP") inside the bankruptcy case and prove that paying the loan would be an undue hardship.

The undue hardship standard is set out in case law, most prominently Brunner v. New York State Higher Education Services Corp., 831 F.2d 395 (2d Cir. 1987), which most circuits follow.

What the Brunner test asks

The Brunner test has three parts. The debtor has to show all three:

1. Minimal standard of living

If the debtor were forced to repay the loans, they could not maintain a "minimal" standard of living for themselves and any dependents, based on current income and expenses.

2. Persistence

The hardship is likely to persist for a significant portion of the loan repayment period — not just a temporary setback.

3. Good faith

The debtor has made good-faith efforts to repay — for example, exploring repayment plans, deferments, or income-driven repayment.

The November 2022 DOJ guidance

On November 17, 2022, the Department of Justice and Department of Education issued joint guidance reframing how the U.S. attests (or stipulates) to undue hardship in § 523(a)(8) APs against federal student loans. The guidance introduced a streamlined attestation process and clearer internal criteria for when DOJ would not contest a discharge.

The empirical effect has been measurable: filings of § 523(a)(8) APs surged after the guidance. The Open Bankruptcy Project's empirical research dataset shows roughly a sevenfold increase in post-guidance AP filings versus pre-guidance, across the same set of U.S. bankruptcy courts.

Federal vs private student loans

Loan typeDischarge pathNotes
Federal direct loans, FFEL, Perkins § 523(a)(8) AP against U.S. Department of Education or servicer Post-Nov-2022 guidance has made this path materially more accessible.
Private student loans § 523(a)(8) AP against the lender; in some cases, may not be dischargeable at all Some private "education loans" may not meet § 523(a)(8)'s definition and may be dischargeable without an AP. The line is fact-specific.

Alternate paths to discharge (without bankruptcy)

Bankruptcy is one path. For federal loans, several non-bankruptcy programs may achieve loan elimination without filing a § 523(a)(8) AP:

Total & Permanent Disability (TPD) Discharge For borrowers with documented total and permanent disability. Administered by the Department of Education, no court filing required. Closed School Discharge Available to borrowers whose schools closed during or shortly after enrollment, where they could not complete the program elsewhere. False Certification Discharge Available where a school falsely certified a borrower's eligibility for federal student aid — for example, ability-to-benefit fraud.

Pro se vs represented

Filing a § 523(a)(8) AP without a lawyer (pro se) is procedurally available but legally complex. The post-2022 DOJ guidance made the process more navigable for pro se filers in some respects, but circuit-level variation in the Brunner test still requires careful research. Many bankruptcy attorneys will not include § 523(a)(8) APs in a standard Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 representation without a separate fee agreement; some will not handle them at all.

If you are looking for help: federal bankruptcy clerks' offices have free pro se filing guides; many state bar associations offer lawyer referral services; law school clinics in many jurisdictions handle student-loan discharge cases at no cost. The Department of Education's ombudsman also handles certain disputes outside of bankruptcy.
