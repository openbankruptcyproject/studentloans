How to Find Legal Help with Student Loan Discharge

Free and reduced-fee legal resources for borrowers considering a § 523(a)(8) adversary proceeding or other student-loan-discharge path. None of these are endorsements; verify current availability and qualifications directly.

Law school clinics

Many U.S. law schools operate consumer bankruptcy and/or student-loan clinics that take pro bono cases. Clinics typically have geographic and case-type limits but often handle § 523(a)(8) APs at no cost to qualifying clients. Searches that often work:

  • "[Your state] law school student loan clinic"
  • "[Your state] law school consumer bankruptcy clinic"
  • The Project on Predatory Student Lending (Harvard Law School) maintains a list of similar clinical programs and projects nationwide.

Legal aid organizations

State and county legal aid organizations often have a consumer-debt practice. Even where they do not handle § 523(a)(8) APs directly, they can help with the threshold bankruptcy filing itself or refer to attorneys who handle the AP. Find your local organization through the Legal Services Corporation locator.

State bar referral services

Most state bar associations operate lawyer-referral services. Some include reduced-fee or sliding-scale options for borrowers below specified income thresholds. Search "[your state] bar lawyer referral service."

Department of Education ombudsman

For non-bankruptcy disputes about federal student loans — servicer errors, repayment plan disputes, application status — the Department of Education's ombudsman handles informal resolution. The ombudsman is not a substitute for bankruptcy counsel but can sometimes resolve issues that would otherwise become AP-trigger facts.

Contact: FSA Ombudsman dispute page on studentaid.gov.

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

For private student loan disputes (where the Department of Education ombudsman doesn't apply), the CFPB accepts complaints about servicer conduct. The complaint goes through the CFPB's portal and the lender/servicer is required to respond.

Submit at consumerfinance.gov/complaint.

State attorney general consumer protection units

Most state attorneys general operate consumer protection units that handle complaints about student-loan servicers, debt collectors, and (in some cases) law firms representing themselves as student-loan-resolution services. State AG action is sometimes more effective than federal enforcement on the servicer/collector side.

Bankruptcy attorneys

For paid representation, consider these search dimensions:

  • Specialization: "[Your state] bankruptcy attorney student loan discharge" — many bankruptcy attorneys do not handle § 523(a)(8) APs and will say so up front.
  • Bar association directories: NACBA (National Association of Consumer Bankruptcy Attorneys) maintains a member directory at nacba.org.
  • Fee structure: § 523(a)(8) AP work is usually billed separately from a standard bankruptcy filing. Get fee structure in writing.
  • Track record: Some attorneys publish their § 523(a)(8) outcomes; others will discuss representative case histories during a consultation.

Self-help books and free guides

Several published guides specifically address § 523(a)(8) APs and pro se procedure. The Department of Education itself has published guidance on the post-2022 attestation process. Check public-library catalogs and the Department of Education bankruptcy page for current resources.

What this site provides

The Open Bankruptcy Project offers educational reference materials and an open empirical dataset. We do not provide legal advice, do not represent clients, and do not refer specific attorneys. Our resources can supplement (but not substitute for) any of the help paths above.
