The SAVE Plan and Bankruptcy Dischargeability

Saving on a Valuable Education (SAVE) was the Biden-era income-driven repayment (IDR) plan launched in August 2023. SAVE was substantially limited by federal court injunctions in 2024 and is in litigation-paused status as of early 2026. This page covers what SAVE was, where it stands, and how IDR enrollment interacts with bankruptcy discharge under § 523(a)(8).

What SAVE was designed to do

SAVE replaced the prior REPAYE plan and added borrower-favorable terms:

  • Discretionary income calculated at 225% of the federal poverty line (vs 150% in older IDR plans), reducing payments substantially
  • Undergraduate borrowers paid 5% of discretionary income (vs 10%); planned for 2024
  • Interest waiver if monthly payment did not cover accrued interest
  • Forgiveness after 10-25 years depending on original loan balance

The litigation paused SAVE

In June 2024, federal courts in the Eighth Circuit and the Tenth Circuit issued injunctions on parts of SAVE based on the Missouri v. Biden line of challenges. The Eighth Circuit's August 2024 stay halted the lower-payment and forgiveness provisions. Borrowers enrolled in SAVE were placed in administrative forbearance — no payments due, no interest accruing — while litigation proceeded.

As of early 2026, the program remains in this paused state. Borrowers should monitor their servicer accounts and studentaid.gov for status changes.

How IDR enrollment affects a bankruptcy AP

This is the core practical question for bankruptcy filers: does enrolling in SAVE (or any IDR plan) help or hurt a § 523(a)(8) adversary proceeding?

Brunner-test perspective

The Brunner test asks whether (1) you cannot maintain a minimal standard of living while paying the loan, (2) the inability is likely to persist, and (3) you have made good-faith efforts to repay. IDR enrollment can affect each prong.

  • Prong 1: If your IDR payment is $0 because of low income, you can argue this itself shows the loan is unaffordable at any positive payment.
  • Prong 2: A persistently $0 IDR payment over years can support the persistence prong.
  • Prong 3: IDR enrollment is generally treated as evidence of good faith, not bad faith. You're not refusing to pay; you're paying what the income-based formula calls for.

Some courts have held that an indefinite $0 IDR payment itself satisfies the undue-hardship standard, on the theory that being forced to remain in IDR for 25 years to obtain forgiveness is a hardship. Other courts disagree. Circuit-level variation matters.

The November 17, 2022 DOJ/Ed guidance

The DOJ and Department of Education issued joint guidance instructing the government to attest to undue hardship in cases that meet specific criteria, including persistent low income relative to the federal poverty line. Borrowers in IDR with $0 or near-$0 payments due to low income are often a fit for attestation.

The guidance is administrative, not statutory. A future administration could withdraw it. Filings under the guidance have grown sharply since November 2022, and the empirical pattern is documented at 523a8.openbankruptcyproject.org.
