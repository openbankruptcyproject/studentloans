Income-Driven Repayment and Bankruptcy

Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) plans and § 523(a)(8) discharge solve different problems, but they interact in important ways. This page explains how IDR fits into the Brunner test and when each path makes sense.

What IDR is

Income-Driven Repayment is a family of federal student loan repayment plans that cap monthly payments at a percentage of discretionary income (typically 5%–20%, depending on plan and family size) and forgive any remaining balance after 20–25 years of qualifying payments. Major plans include:

  • SAVE (Saving on a Valuable Education) — the newest plan; 5% of discretionary income for undergraduate loans, 10% for graduate, with shorter forgiveness windows for low-balance borrowers. (Note: SAVE has been the subject of litigation; check current status on studentaid.gov.)
  • PAYE (Pay As You Earn) — 10% of discretionary income, 20-year forgiveness
  • IBR (Income-Based Repayment) — 10% or 15% depending on borrowing date, 20- or 25-year forgiveness
  • ICR (Income-Contingent Repayment) — the oldest plan, generally less favorable than newer options

IDR is not a discharge. It is a repayment-plan option that reduces monthly obligations and (eventually) writes off remaining balance after the qualifying period.

How IDR interacts with the Brunner test

Courts examining § 523(a)(8) APs under the Brunner test routinely consider whether the debtor has applied for IDR. Failure to apply for or use IDR has historically been treated as evidence against the third Brunner prong (good-faith repayment efforts). After the Nov 2022 DOJ guidance, this question is approached somewhat differently — the guidance recognizes that IDR isn't always practical, and DOJ may stipulate to discharge even where IDR was not exhausted — but the legal standard hasn't changed.

For private loans (where DOJ guidance does not apply), Brunner courts continue to treat non-use of available repayment programs (whatever the lender offers) as a third-prong factor.

When IDR is the better path than discharge

  • The borrower's income is irregular but not catastrophically low — IDR's income-driven feature can keep payments at $0 in low-income months without requiring an AP.
  • The borrower expects income recovery within a few years — the persistence prong of Brunner is hard to meet for short-term hardships.
  • The 20- or 25-year forgiveness window is acceptable to the borrower — eventual write-off without bankruptcy.

When discharge is the better path than IDR

  • The hardship is long-term (disability, advanced age with limited remaining earning years, permanent low-earning capacity).
  • Loans were taken for a program the borrower could not benefit from (poor school quality, fraud, identity theft — here, alternate-path discharges may apply).
  • IDR has been tried and the borrower remains unable to maintain a minimum standard of living even on IDR-reduced payments.
  • The borrower already has 20+ years of payments behind them and the forgiveness horizon is long enough that finishing IDR isn't realistic.

The "tax bomb" concern

Historically, IDR forgiveness at the end of the 20- or 25-year period was treated as taxable income to the borrower — a potentially large tax liability in the year of forgiveness. Recent legislation extended the tax-free treatment of IDR forgiveness through December 31, 2025 (and possibly beyond). The tax-treatment landscape continues to evolve; borrowers planning around long-horizon IDR forgiveness should check the current rule before relying on tax-free outcomes.

Borrower Defense and Public Service Loan Forgiveness are separate non-bankruptcy discharge pathways for federal loans. They are conceptually closer to a discharge than IDR is, because they eliminate the remaining balance in less than 20 years. See PSLF and bankruptcy for the related PSLF analysis.

If you have a § 523(a)(8) AP pending and IDR comes up as a question

Counsel typically advises against unilaterally enrolling in IDR mid-AP unless directed, because the act of enrolling can affect how the court evaluates the Brunner factors. If you are representing yourself pro se, the safest default is to document any IDR application history (approval, denial, payment history) and bring it to the AP. Don't change repayment status during the AP without thinking it through.
