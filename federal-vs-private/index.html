Federal vs Private Student Loans in Bankruptcy

Federal and private student loans look identical when they're taking money out of your paycheck, but they go through different bankruptcy paths. Knowing which kind you have matters before you do anything else.

The basic distinction

How to identify which kind you have

If you're not sure, two free authoritative sources will tell you:

  • For federal loans: Log into studentaid.gov. The "My Aid" section lists every federal loan tied to your Social Security number, with current balance, servicer, and loan type (Direct, FFEL, Perkins).
  • For private loans: Pull a free credit report from annualcreditreport.com. Private student loans appear on the credit report under "installment loans" with the lender's name.

If a loan appears on your credit report but not on studentaid.gov, it's private. If it's on studentaid.gov, it's federal.

The "qualified educational loan" question for private loans

Not all loans called "student loans" by their lenders meet the Bankruptcy Code's definition of qualified educational loan in § 523(a)(8)(B). Loans that fail the qualification test may be dischargeable in bankruptcy without a § 523(a)(8) AP — they go through the regular dismissal of unsecured debt.

The qualification test asks, among other things, whether the loan was made for "qualified higher education expenses" at an "eligible educational institution" as defined by the Internal Revenue Code. A few categories of private student lending may fall outside:

  • Loans made to a student attending a non-Title-IV school (one not eligible for federal financial aid)
  • Loans made for cost-of-attendance items not within the IRS definition of qualified educational expenses
  • Loans not certified by the school
  • Loans for non-degree programs at certain institutions

The Second, Fifth, Tenth, and Eleventh Circuits have all decided cases finding specific private student loans not qualified educational loans and therefore dischargeable without an undue-hardship showing. This is a fact-specific legal question and worth raising explicitly with bankruptcy counsel.

Mixed federal-and-private situations

Many borrowers have both. The two paths run independently:

  • The federal portion benefits from the Nov 2022 DOJ guidance and the alternate non-bankruptcy paths.
  • The private portion requires its own § 523(a)(8) AP (or, where the qualification question applies, a determination that the loans aren't covered by § 523(a)(8) at all).

An AP must be filed against each defendant. Bankruptcy courts treat each separately on its own merits.

Practical first step: Pull both records (studentaid.gov + a credit report). Make a list of every loan: lender, balance, servicer, and which category it falls into. The list itself is what you bring to a bankruptcy attorney consultation. The path forward depends on which kinds of loans you're dealing with.

If you're filing pro se

The federal-vs-private distinction is the first thing the bankruptcy court will want to understand about your discharge claim. Listing each loan correctly on your schedules and in any adversary proceeding complaint is essential.
