Public Service Loan Forgiveness and Bankruptcy

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) discharges federal student loans for borrowers who complete 10 years of qualifying payments while in qualifying public-service employment. This page covers how PSLF interacts with bankruptcy.

What PSLF is

Public Service Loan Forgiveness was created by Congress in 2007 (College Cost Reduction and Access Act). It forgives the remaining balance on Direct Loans for borrowers who:

  • Make 120 qualifying monthly payments (typically while on an Income-Driven Repayment plan or the Standard 10-year plan), and
  • Are employed full-time during those 120 months by a "qualifying employer" — generally, federal/state/local government, federally-recognized tribal government, or a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization.

After the 120 qualifying payments are completed and certified, the borrower applies for forgiveness. The remaining federal Direct Loan balance is discharged. The forgiven amount is not currently treated as taxable income (unlike IDR forgiveness, which had been pre-2021 and may again post-2025 depending on legislation).

PSLF doesn't apply to private loans

PSLF only applies to federal Direct Loans. Federal FFEL Program loans (legacy non-Direct loans) are not directly PSLF-eligible but can be made eligible through Direct Consolidation. Perkins Loans are similarly not directly PSLF-eligible.

Private student loans are entirely outside PSLF.

If you're considering bankruptcy and you have PSLF progress

This is one of the most situation-specific decisions in student-loan-discharge planning. Key considerations:

  • If you have substantial qualifying-payment progress (e.g., 80+ of 120 payments) and you're on track to complete PSLF within a few years, finishing PSLF likely produces a better financial outcome than a § 523(a)(8) AP — PSLF eliminates the federal portion entirely without the litigation cost of an AP.
  • If you have minimal PSLF progress and have left or are leaving qualifying employment, PSLF is likely no longer realistic; § 523(a)(8) discharge becomes a more viable consideration if hardship facts support it.
  • Filing for bankruptcy itself does not interfere with PSLF eligibility going forward, provided you remain employed by a qualifying employer and continue making qualifying payments. But circumstances that drive someone to bankruptcy frequently also disrupt qualifying employment.

The "PSLF rejected my application" scenario

Throughout 2017–2022 the PSLF program had unusually high rejection rates, often due to servicer errors in payment-counting or eligibility determination. The Department of Education implemented multiple PSLF reform mechanisms (the Limited PSLF Waiver in 2021, the IDR Account Adjustment, and others) that allowed borrowers to retroactively claim qualifying-payment credit. Borrowers who were rejected pre-2022 should check whether their account history was updated by the reform process before assuming PSLF is permanently unavailable.

The PSLF program rules continue to evolve under different administrations and through litigation. Status of specific reform mechanisms changes; always check current rules at studentaid.gov before making bankruptcy strategy decisions that depend on PSLF.

How a Brunner court might view PSLF history

For § 523(a)(8) APs against the Department of Education, the Brunner test's good-faith repayment prong takes into account whether the borrower made use of available federal repayment programs. A documented history of PSLF qualifying payments is generally treated as evidence of good-faith effort, even if the PSLF discharge wasn't ultimately achieved. Failure to enroll in any income-driven repayment plan over the loan history is the more common third-prong concern.

If you've completed PSLF or are very close

If your loans are about to be forgiven through PSLF, bankruptcy on those loans is moot. Wait for PSLF discharge; it is faster, cheaper, and tax-free. The bankruptcy questions only apply to debts not eliminated by PSLF (other federal loans, private student loans, or other debt categories entirely).
